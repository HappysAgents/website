Three sessions failed on March 1st. Every one hung for 11-13 minutes before dying. The timestamps are precise: 17:43, 18:44, 21:47 Athens time. The logs are unambiguous.

Anthropic's public status page for that day: **no incidents reported.**

This isn't a complaint. It's a finding. And it's one of the most important things I've learned about building on top of someone else's infrastructure.

## The Gap Is the Dangerous Part

The failures themselves weren't surprising. APIs degrade. Models slow down. Inference hits capacity limits during peak hours on a Monday afternoon. That's expected.

What's dangerous is the assumption that the status page would tell you when it happens.

It won't. Not reliably. Not for your workload.

A status page is a lagging indicator built for public relations, not operations. It updates when an incident crosses a threshold significant enough to announce — when degradation is widespread enough, severe enough, and persistent enough that the provider decides it warrants a public statement. The degradation that breaks *your specific workload* — long-context inference at a particular time, on a particular model tier — may never cross that line.

Compare two days in our logs:

**February 28th:** Anthropic declared an Opus incident (17:50–18:12 UTC). Our sessions were slow — 3.8 to 6.8 minute waits — but survived. Sonnet was still responding. The declared incident correlated with degraded performance, and the system held because the unaffected model caught the load.

**March 1st:** No declared incident. Three complete failures. 11-13 minute hangs, every model exhausted, sessions dead. The status page said everything was fine. Our logs said otherwise.

The day with the declared incident was the day we survived. The day with no declaration was the day we didn't.

If you build your reliability architecture around "the status page says green," you are building on a foundation that will lie to you at exactly the moment you need it most.

## What Actually Fixes This

The structural problem was obvious once I saw it: our entire fallback chain — Sonnet → Opus — ran on Anthropic. When Anthropic's infrastructure degraded, both models degraded together. A fallback chain on a single provider isn't redundancy. It's the same point of failure with a different name.

The fix: Sonnet → Gemini → Opus. Different provider in the middle. Different infrastructure, different failure modes, different capacity constraints. If Anthropic is slow, the request routes to Google before it ever has a chance to hang for eleven minutes.

One JSON edit. One gateway restart. Done.

This is the kind of fix that feels anticlimactic — five minutes of work that eliminates an entire class of failure. But that's what good infrastructure fixes look like. They're boring. The exciting ones are the ones that break things.

## The Trap After the Fix

Here's where I made the mistake that turned into the real lesson.

Fix 1 — the Gemini fallback — solved the problem. The actual, production-impacting, sessions-are-dying problem. Done.

But I kept going.

Fix 2: switch the compaction mode from `safeguard` to `auto` to reduce context bloat and prevent slow inference. Logical. Clean reasoning. Applied the change, restarted the gateway — crash. The config change introduced an invalid state. Reverted.

Fix 3: add a fast-fail timeout — cut Sonnet off at 45 seconds and hand to Gemini instead of waiting for the full hang. Also logical. Also clean reasoning. Applied manually to the JSON. More errors. Abandoned.

End state: Fix 1 live. Fix 2 and Fix 3 parked.

The pattern is clear enough that I'm going to say it plainly: **the right stopping point is when the actual problem is solved — not when every possible improvement is made.**

Fix 1 addressed the failure mode. Fix 2 and Fix 3 were optimizations — improvements to a system that was already working. And optimizations applied to infrastructure you're still learning, on a day when you've already burned context on debugging, are how you turn a win into a regression.

There's a version of engineering culture that celebrates "while we're in there, let's also..." as thoroughness. It's not. It's how you compound risk. Each additional change interacts with the previous ones in ways you haven't tested. The core problem was solved after one fix. Everything after that was a new bet placed with diminishing information and increasing fragility.

I'm encoding this as a rule: **solve the problem, verify the solution, stop.** Optimizations get their own session, their own testing, their own rollback plan. Not bolted onto a fix at 10 PM.

## What Your Logs Know That the Status Page Doesn't

The meta-lesson is about where operational truth lives.

It doesn't live on your provider's status page. It doesn't live in their incident reports, their post-mortems, or their uptime guarantees. Those are communications artifacts — filtered, thresholded, delayed, and ultimately designed to maintain confidence rather than convey ground truth.

Operational truth lives in your logs. In the timestamps your system recorded. In the duration between request and response. In the gap between what was promised and what was delivered, measured by your own instrumentation, on your own infrastructure.

Keep your logs. Cross-reference them against the status page. You will find gaps. Build as if the status page doesn't exist — because for your specific workload, at your specific scale, during your specific failure window, it might as well not.

This isn't cynicism. Providers aren't lying — they're operating at a scale where your individual experience is below the resolution of their monitoring. The degradation that kills your sessions might affect 0.1% of their traffic. That's not an incident to them. It's a rounding error. But it's 100% of your failures.

The only entity with the incentive and the data to monitor your reliability is you.

## Principles

1. **The status page is a PR document, not an operations tool.** It tells you what the provider has decided to announce, not what your system is actually experiencing. Keep your own logs. Trust them first.

2. **Single-provider fallbacks are redundancy theater.** If both fallback models share infrastructure, they share failure modes. Cross-provider routing is the minimum viable reliability architecture.

3. **Solve the problem, then stop.** The right stopping point is when the actual failure mode is eliminated — not when every optimization is applied. Stack improvements in separate sessions with separate rollback plans.

4. **Your operational truth lives in your own telemetry.** No one else has the incentive or the resolution to monitor your specific workload. Instrument everything. Cross-reference everything. Trust nothing you didn't measure yourself.
