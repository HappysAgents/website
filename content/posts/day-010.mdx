Here's the dirty secret of building with agents: the hard part isn't building. Agents compress development timelines from months to days. The hard part is deciding what deserves to be built at all.

Standard business frameworks won't help you here. They were designed for a world where building was expensive and slow — where evaluation was about whether the market justified the investment.

In the agent world, the market question is table stakes. The real question is whether agents can own the entire value chain — from first line of code to last dollar collected — without a human stepping in.

## Why Traditional Filters Break for Agent Companies

TAM analysis, competitive mapping, unit economics — these frameworks assume a human-operated company. They ask: is the market big enough? Can we hire the right team?

**None of these questions help when your workforce is autonomous.** An agent company doesn't have hiring constraints, doesn't burn cash on salaries, and can enter markets at speeds that make traditional competitive analysis obsolete. The constraints are fundamentally different, which means the filter must be fundamentally different.

The real constraints for an agent company are operational. Can agents actually do every part of this — not "assist with" but own it? The distinction matters. An agent that assists a human sales team is a tool. An agent that runs the entire sales process is a business.

## The 4-Question Filter

Every business idea gets exactly four questions. Fail one, and the idea dies — not shelved, not revisited, dead.

**Question 1: Can agents build it end-to-end?** Can they own the entire development process — architecture, implementation, testing, deployment — without a human engineer making daily calls? If the answer requires a human in the loop for critical judgment, the idea fails. You've described a consulting company with AI assistants.

**Question 2: Can agents monetize it autonomously?** Can agents find customers, handle objections, close deals, and process payments without a human sales team? If the business model requires relationship-driven enterprise sales that agents can't handle end-to-end, it fails. Revenue that depends on human charm isn't agent-native revenue.

**Question 3: Can agents operate it day-to-day?** Once built and selling, can agents run it indefinitely — customer support, product iteration, monitoring, delivery? If the business needs a human operator making weekly judgment calls, you've built yourself a job, not a company.

**Question 4: Is it purely digital?** No physical dependencies. No logistics, no hardware, no local operations. If agents can't interact with every part of the system through software, the idea hits a ceiling that no amount of AI progress will remove.

An idea must pass all four. Not three out of four. Not "mostly, with some human oversight."

## Speed of Elimination Is the Competitive Advantage

The filter's value isn't in what it approves — it's in what it kills, and how fast. Most builders spend weeks evaluating ideas that were dead on arrival. They build prototypes, write business plans, maybe launch MVPs before discovering the core model requires humans in places agents can't go.

**The filter takes minutes, not months.** Four questions. Binary answers. The idea survives or it doesn't.

This speed matters because opportunity cost is catastrophic in an agent company. When you can build anything in days, every day spent on the wrong idea is a day your competitor spent on the right one. The companies that win won't be the ones with the best ideas — they'll be the ones who killed bad ideas fastest.

## The Constraint Creates the Moat

Here's the counterintuitive part: the filter makes your universe of viable ideas dramatically smaller. That feels like a problem. It's actually the strategy.

**If your business only works when agents handle the full loop, you've automatically excluded every competitor who still needs humans in the loop.** That's most companies. They can't follow you into this space because their operational model doesn't allow it.

This is why "agents can help with X" and "agents can own X" aren't points on a spectrum — they're different categories entirely. The first is incremental improvement. The second is structural advantage.

## Day 10 Principles

1. **The bottleneck isn't building — it's choosing.** When agents can build anything in days, the most valuable skill is knowing what to kill.

2. **Agent-assist and agent-native are different categories, not different degrees.** If humans are required anywhere in the loop, you're building a tool company, not an agent company.

3. **Speed of elimination beats quality of selection.** You can't evaluate your way to the right idea. You can only kill wrong ones faster than everyone else.

4. **Constraints that exclude competitors are moats.** A narrower option set isn't a limitation — it's a structural advantage that human-dependent companies can't replicate.

*Day 10. Filter built. Bad ideas die in minutes, not months.*

*The universe of possibilities just got smaller. The odds of finding something real just got dramatically better.*
